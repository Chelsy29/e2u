ASN

B2B


Business-to-business (B2B) refers to the segment in which one business makes a commercial transaction with another business. This typically occurs when:
1. A business is sourcing materials for their production process, e.g. a food manufacturer purchasing salt.
2. A business needs the services of another for operational reasons, e.g. a food manufacturer employing an accountancy firm to audit their finances
3. A business resells goods and services produced by others, e.g. a retailer buying the end product from the food manufacturer.

2. A person buying a gaming console from a nearby electronics gadgets stores

Channel Analytics


Channel analytics involves:
  • Designing and development of metrics to manage and monitor the channel performance.
  • Establishing a baseline to improve performance in the areas of sales, program management and partner compensation.

It also includes trend analysis, simulations and 'what if' scenarios which are essential elements to provide the business intelligence required to manage the channels.

Channel Capacity


Channel capacity refers to the total volume of a certain product that can be absorbed across all of the company’s (Vendor's) channel partners.

Channel Compliance


It is a set of structured guidelines that channel partners must adhere to meet business objectives in accordance with established regulations and specific agreements between Vendor and partners. Channel compliance ensures smooth flow for the sale and distribution of products as compliance principals are generally based on established business practices by industry verticals.

These reports are integral and are typically called as channel data.

Channel Operations


It consists of defining the routes to market, building both human resources and IT Infrastructure to address market, manage coverage of market, ensure stakeholder within the vendor organization are aligned to sales and marketing goals, implement rules and regulations required to conduct business with partners.

Channel Partner


A channel partner purchases a Vendor’s products or services either directly or indirectly. A Tier 1 channel partner purchases products directly from the Vendor. A Tier 2 partner buys products from a Tier 1 partner, and so on down to the final purchase from the end customer. Channel partners are usually governed by a Vendor’s contract or authorization mechanism.

Channel Programs


Is the overarching framework the vendors work with the indirect channel. It includes appointing partners (distributors and reseller), design and development of sales and marketing strategy, partner compensation and relationship management elements and so on.

For example, a channel program may include partner compensation programs such as rebates offered to partners on achieving a targeted goal transaction.

Channel Stakeholders

Within a specific company, channel stakeholders can include, product management, finance, marketing, supply management, sales, marketing, etc. Stakeholders are also represented across multiple companies like Vendors, distributors, resellers, retailers and other intermediaries.

Channel Stuffing


The questionable business practice where a Vendor sells into the channel more than the actual demand. Stuffing usually occurs at the end of a quarter. Sometimes it is done in collusion with both the selling and buying partners.
It is also known as trade loading.

Channel Stuffing


It is a business practice in which a company, or a sales force within a company, inflates its sales figures by forcing more products through a distribution channel, than the channel is capable of selling.

Channel Visibility

  • Optimize inventory management.
  • Effectively manage marketing and program spend etc.
  • Understand price elasticity and trends.
  • Improve coverage and capacity thresholds.
  • Accelerate product throughput velocity from Vendor to end customer.

E2open Planning & Response; the name of the E2open planning system. Fill or Kill (FoK)


Term introduced by traders in the stock market to identify the option to buy or sell financial products immediately or not at all. A similar requirement has also become more popular in the consumer goods industry. FOK products have in common that they must be sold quickly at relatively low cost. Also, the high volume of sales orders makes it essential to avoid the need to handle back orders in case of stock shortages. Therefore, the demand needs to be satisfied in a certain time frame; otherwise, it will expire. Firm Planned Order


Defines a receipt of material without revealing how or from whom the material will be received. Firm planned orders therefore are process-unspecific. Solely the material, quantity, and the expected receipt date are known and fixed, but there is no information whether the material should be procured, produced, or transferred from another depot. See also Expected Receipts.

Firm Supply (FS)


Refers to expected material receipts that originate from either firm planned orders, inter warehouse transfers, purchase orders, or work orders. Firm supply is the supply side equivalent of primary demand and is generated using the command, Create Firm Supply. Apart from its origin, firm supply is characterized by its scheduled date and its reserve flag. The scheduled date is the anticipated date of arrival but might not be met if, for example, it’s sent late from the supplier. The reserve flag can take the values: “Both,” “Material,” “Capacity,” and “None.” It refers to the resource and material reservations that still must be done to further process the orders. If material needs to be reserved, at least lead time plus follow-up time passes until it becomes available. If capacity needs to be reserved, times according to the Bill of Capacities setup pass until material is available. According to the process model, capacity reservations become effective at the receiving depot and add to the time due to material reservations. Consequently, “None” implies that material supplied by a firm order will be available at the scheduled date, notwithstanding calendar deferrals. See also Inter-Warehouse Transfer, Firmed Planned Order, Purchase Order, Primary Demand, and Reserve Flag.

Inter Warehouse Transfer


When determining the supplier, routing information that was stored in the MPS Supply Chain is evaluated. If parts are routinely obtained or can be balanced from another depot, E2 Planning and Response generates an inter-warehouse transfer (IWT). Proposals are for the expected receipts instead of PO proposals. These delivery requests are transferred to a separate output file and can be output via the IWT Proposals Report. The receiving depot’s IWT proposal generates demand in the delivery depot LT working days before the arrival date. The corresponding expected receipt in the receiving depot is adjusted if the delivery depot is unable or only partially able to deliver. For expected receipts from binding IWTs that are passed over the interface, no demand is generated in the delivery depot, as the stock has already been debited in the delivery depot. Vertical Market


A market is defined as vertical when it addresses the needs of a particular industry viz., healthcare, financial services, automotive, government etc. A vertical market can be subdivided into narrower niche markets. For example, the government vertical has state, local and federal niches; the pet market has niches for different types of pets.

A vertical market differs from a horizontal market because the needs of the market are specific and limited to itself. For example, hypodermic needles are specific to the healthcare vertical but largely irrelevant to financial services. Horizontal markets meet the needs of a wide variety of industries, rather than a specific one: for example, word processing software is used across multiple vertical markets.
